Deontological structure tells the agent what to do; virtue ethics asks
what kind of agent to be. This chapter develops the Aristotelian
tradition within the categorical framework: virtuous character is a
stable fixed point of the habituation functor, the doctrine of the mean
is constrained optimization on the virtue sheaf, practical wisdom is
local section selection, and flourishing is the global section of a
well-lived life. The Flourishing Theorem (VII.T34) shows that
eudaimonia exists precisely when virtuous activity glues coherently
across all life-stages. Virtue and deontology are complementary, not
rival: the categorical imperative provides duties, virtue provides the
character to satisfy them reliably.
