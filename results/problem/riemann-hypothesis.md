## Overview

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH) asserts that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function lie on the critical line <math><mrow><mtext>Re</mtext><mo>(</mo><mi>s</mi><mo>)</mo><mo>=</mo><mfrac><mn>1</mn><mn>2</mn></mfrac></mrow></math>. It is one of the seven Clay Millennium Problems and arguably the most important unsolved problem in mathematics. The framework approaches RH through the [Spectral Algebra]({{ '/framework/mathematics-spectral-algebra/' | relative_url }}): spectral purity of the <math><mi>&zeta;</mi></math>-function in the B/C classifier of the [lemniscate]({{ '/framework/mathematics-prime-polarity/' | relative_url }}) boundary.

## Detail

Within Category <math><mi>&tau;</mi></math>, the Riemann zeta function becomes a <math><mi>&tau;</mi></math>-morphism on the lemniscate boundary. The [spectral trichotomy]({{ '/framework/mathematics-spectral-algebra/' | relative_url }}) (III.T14) classifies every boundary character as B-supported, C-supported, or X-mixing. The tau-effective RH statement is that the zeta function's spectral decomposition is *pure* — all spectral weight lies on the critical axis of the B/C classification. The tau-gap for the temporal force (the spectral analogue of RH) is proved (III.T27), but the full bridge to the orthodox RH statement remains conjectural.
