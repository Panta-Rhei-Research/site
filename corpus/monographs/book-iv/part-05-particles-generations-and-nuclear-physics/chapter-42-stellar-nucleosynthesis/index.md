Stars are cosmic forges that transmute hydrogen into the full periodic table across successive burning stages. Big Bang nucleosynthesis first set the primordial mix: ~75% H and ~25% He by mass (structurally 61/81 and 20/81, with a 5/6 domain-wall correction), with everything heavier than lithium left to stellar production. The Sun's pp-chain converts 4p → ⁴He + 26.7 MeV over 4.6 billion years; more massive stars run the CNO cycle (rate ∝ T¹⁶). When hydrogen is exhausted, helium burning produces carbon via the triple-α process through the Hoyle resonance, then oxygen by α-capture. In massive stars (M > 8 M☉) successive burning stages—carbon (500 yr), neon (1 yr), oxygen (6 months), silicon (1 day)—build an onion-shell structure culminating in an iron core. When the core exceeds the Chandrasekhar mass it collapses in ~1 s, launching a core-collapse supernova; the shock and neutrino wind provide conditions for the r-process. Type Ia supernovae (white-dwarf thermonuclear runaway) are the primary cosmic iron source, each producing ~0.6 M☉ of ⁵⁶Ni → ⁵⁶Fe. Each stellar generation enriches the interstellar medium, driving monotonically increasing metallicity from Population III to the Sun. The τ³ perspective: stellar nucleosynthesis is T² gradient flow toward the iron saturation optimum.
