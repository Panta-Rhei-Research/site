The Heisenberg uncertainty principle is standardly presented as a fundamental limit on knowledge, sometimes attributed to measurement disturbance. In τ³ it is a theorem about geometry. Position-like operators X̂ measure angular coordinates on the T² fiber; momentum-like operators P̂ measure the rate of worldline evolution along τ¹; and the CR-structure irreversibly couples these two sectors, giving [X̂, P̂] = iκ where κ is a geometric constant determined by the CR-curvature and the coupling ι_τ = 2/(π + e). From this commutator the uncertainty bound ΔX · ΔP ≥ κ/2 follows by the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality on H_τ—no measurement or observer is involved. What appears as an electron cloud is a projection effect: the underlying toroidal geometry is well-defined and deterministic; the "uncertainty" is how periodic patterns on T² map onto spherical coordinates. The geometric constant κ differs from the measured ℏ because no physical units have yet been fixed; calibration via the neutron (Chapter 20) converts κ → ℏ.
