Carbon's unique position in chemistry follows from three reinforcing properties: four valence electrons (halfway to a filled octet), three available hybridisation states (sp³ tetrahedral, sp² trigonal planar, sp linear), and catenation — C–C bonds at 347 kJ/mol strong enough to sustain chains of arbitrary length under ambient conditions. No other element matches this: silicon's Si–Si bond (226 kJ/mol) is weaker, and silicon preferentially forms much stronger Si–O bonds, producing oxides rather than chains. The result is over ten million known organic compounds, dwarfing the roughly 500,000 inorganic species.

The chapter surveys the three hydrocarbon families: alkanes (C_nH_{2n+2}, sp³, all σ bonds, free rotation), alkenes (C_nH_{2n}, sp², one or more C=C, cis/trans isomerism from rigid π bond), and alkynes (C_nH_{2n-2}, sp, linear geometry at triple bond). Major functional groups — hydroxyl, carboxyl, amine, carbonyl, nitrile — are interpreted as τ² mode decorations on the molecular graph that define local reactivity. Structural isomers share a molecular formula but differ in connectivity; stereoisomers share connectivity but differ in three-dimensional arrangement. Chirality — non-superimposability on a mirror image, arising from a stereogenic carbon with four distinct substituents — has profound biological significance: in the τ³ framework, the two enantiomers are opposite τ² winding orientations on T², topologically distinct and continuous-deformation-inequivalent. Life's universal preference for L-amino acids and D-sugars is therefore a statement about the preferred winding direction of biochemical τ² modes. The aromatic part of the chapter resolves the 19th-century benzene puzzle: all six C–C bonds are identical at 1.40 Å (intermediate between single 1.54 Å and double 1.34 Å) because the six π electrons are delocalised over the entire ring, producing aromatic stabilisation energy ≈ 150 kJ/mol. Hückel's rule — a cyclic, planar, conjugated molecule is aromatic if and only if N_π = 4n+2 — is interpreted as a T² mode quantisation condition: only these π counts produce a fully symmetric mode pattern on the cyclic graph, while the 4n case is frustrated (a node at the Fermi level) and antiaromatic. Heterocyclic aromatics (pyridine, pyrrole, furan) and polycyclic PAHs (naphthalene, anthracene) extend the pattern; the DNA bases are heterocyclic aromatics, making the genetic alphabet an aromatic structure whose stability ensures the structural rigidity required for faithful replication.
