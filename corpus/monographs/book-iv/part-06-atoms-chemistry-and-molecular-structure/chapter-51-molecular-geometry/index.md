Molecular geometry — the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms in a molecule — determines virtually every macroscopic property: melting point, solubility, reactivity, colour, biological function. Two molecules with the same formula C₂H₆O (ethanol and dimethyl ether) differ in boiling point by over 100°C purely because their three-dimensional geometries differ. The Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) model, developed by Sidgwick, Powell, Gillespie, and Nyholm, predicts geometry from a single principle: electron-pair domains around a central atom maximise their mutual angular separation to minimise Pauli–Coulomb repulsion. In Category τ, VSEPR acquires a structural foundation: each electron pair is a τ² mode on the fiber T², and repulsion is the mode exclusion forcing maximal angular separation on the torus surface. Shape is structure; structure is function.

The steric number SN = bonding domains + lone pairs (where a double or triple bond counts as one domain) uniquely determines the electron-pair geometry: linear (SN 2, 180°, e.g. CO₂), trigonal planar (SN 3, 120°, e.g. BF₃), tetrahedral (SN 4, 109.5°, e.g. CH₄), trigonal bipyramidal (SN 5, 90°/120°, e.g. PCl₅), octahedral (SN 6, 90°, e.g. SF₆). Each geometry maximises the minimum angle between adjacent domains for that steric number.

Lone pairs — non-bonding τ² modes localised on the central atom — are not constrained by a second nucleus and spread over a larger angular sector, exerting greater repulsion: LP–LP > LP–BP > BP–BP. This compresses bond angles below the ideal values and produces derived molecular geometries: bent SO₂ (~119°), trigonal pyramidal NH₃ (107.3°), bent H₂O (104.5°), seesaw SF₄, T-shaped ClF₃, square planar XeF₄. Molecular polarity is the vector sum of bond dipoles μ_mol = Σ μᵢ; high molecular symmetry (tetrahedral CCl₄, linear CO₂, trigonal planar BF₃) enforces cancellation, while lone pairs or mixed substituents produce a net polarisation vector. Water's bent geometry (104.5°, dipole 1.85 D, two lone pairs on oxygen) enables hydrogen bonding — the property responsible for water's anomalous heat capacity, ice floating on liquid water, and ultimately the possibility of life. Drug chirality, enzyme lock-and-key recognition, and antigen complementarity are all expressions of three-dimensional τ² mode geometry.
