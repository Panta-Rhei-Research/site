The defect tuple 𝐝 = (d₁, d₂, d₃, d₄) was introduced in Part II to classify individual particles — electrons, protons, neutrons each carrying a specific pattern of mobility, vorticity, compression, and topological winding. Part VII reveals a deeper role for the same object. When a macroscopic sample contains 10²³ defect bundles, the aggregate tuple 𝐝̄ = (1/|J|) Σ 𝐝(fⱼ) encodes the collective phase rather than any single particle. The inequality pattern among the four averaged components — which are zero, which dominate, whether vorticity is quantized — determines the macroscopic regime. One mathematical object, applied at every scale from femtometers to kilometers, classifies all matter. The universality is not metaphorical: it follows from the fact that the four defect components correspond to the four independent geometric modes of the torus T², and those modes are present at every scale of aggregation. What changes between particle physics and fluid dynamics is not the framework but the relative magnitudes and the level at which the ensemble average is taken. Eight structurally distinct inequality patterns exhaust the possible configurations — eight canonical fluid regimes — and phase transitions are precisely the crossings between them.
