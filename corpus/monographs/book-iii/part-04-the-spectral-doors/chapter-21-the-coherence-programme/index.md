Part III ended with nine spectral tools and binding export contracts. Part IV now applies those tools to the eight Millennium Problems — but not problem by problem, without a unifying framework. The governing observation is that every Millennium Problem is an instance of a single three-node correspondence: boundary data, spectral structure, and interior structure are mutually determining. The Mutual Determination Schema formalises this as a bidirectional passage (B) ↔ (I) ↔ (S). For the Riemann Hypothesis: boundary = lemniscate characters χ₊ and χ₋; spectral structure = zeros of ζ_τ on the critical strip; interior = prime distribution encoded in the primorial ladder. For the Poincaré Conjecture: boundary = local Hartogs patches and their transitions; spectral structure = the fundamental group π₁; interior = global topology forced to S³. For P vs NP at E₂: the bidirectionality requires the self-referential structure of codes that contain their own decoders, which is why that problem is deferred to Part IX. The enrichment placement table records all eight assignments with scope grades. The chapter also provides the first live application of the five-question coherence checklist of Chapter 20, working through the RH instantiation to demonstrate how the template calibrates confidence before an argument is made.
