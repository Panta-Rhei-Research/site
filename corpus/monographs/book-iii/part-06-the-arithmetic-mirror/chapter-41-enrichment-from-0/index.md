Part V proved three theorems at enrichment level E₁: positive regularity (*III.T25*, Chapter 34), the Yang–Mills gap (*III.T27*, Chapter 37), and NF-addressability (*III.T28*, Chapter 39). All three were stated as E₁ results, but the enrichment step itself — the passage from E₀ to E₁ — was used implicitly: each chapter assumed the split-complex structure, the sector decomposition, and the defect functional framework without isolating the functor that creates them. This chapter makes the functor explicit. We define the enrichment functor Enr₀₁ (*III.D57*), catalogue the three structural additions it provides, and recast Part V's results as three readings of a single E₁ Mutual Determination instance (*III.D58*, *III.P24*). The chapter closes with a preview of the E₁ → E₂ step that BSD and Langlands will supply in the chapters ahead.

## What this chapter contributes

The enrichment functor (*III.D57*) takes an E₀ spectral algebra — the primorial tower of ℤ/Prim(k)ℤ with its character ring and lemniscate boundary — and equips it with three additions: (i) split-complex dynamics, replacing the scalar coefficient ring ℤ with H_τ = ℤ[j]/(j²−1) and thereby replacing the Laplacian with the hyperbolic wave operator; (ii) sector-coupled processes, activating the 4+1 sector template and tying each sector's spectral content to an admissibility predicate; and (iii) defect functionals, measuring the deviation of each configuration from NF-canonical form and providing the contractivity that the τ-Gap Meta-Theorem requires. No individual Part V chapter defined all three additions in one place; Enr₀₁ does so.

The E₁ Mutual Determination schema (*III.D58*) recasts the enrichment as a bidirectional determination: boundary data (σ-fixed characters, τ-admissible fluid and gauge data) determines interior spectral structure (sectors, gap constants, NF-depth bounds), and interior structure in turn reconstructs canonical boundary assignment. This is not new content but a new frame: the same bidirectional logic that Mutual Determination supplies at E₀ now operates at E₁ with sector resolution.

The Three-Reading Equivalence (*III.P24*) is the chapter's main result: the Navier–Stokes, Yang–Mills, and Hodge results of Part V are shown to be three readings of a single Mutual Determination instance applied to three admissibility classes — τ-admissible fluid data, τ-admissible gauge data, and σ-fixed characters respectively. The proof proceeds by exhibiting a single commutative diagram in which each row is one of the three Part V results and each column is one ingredient of Mutual Determination (boundary, spectral, interior). The unification is τ-internal: it does not assert that the three classical Millennium Problems are equivalent, only that their τ-effective counterparts share a common enrichment template.
