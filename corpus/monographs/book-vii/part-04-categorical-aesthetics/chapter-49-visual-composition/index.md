Visual composition is spatial aesthetics at its most explicit: the arrangement of elements within a two-dimensional frame. This chapter interprets the standard rules of composition — balance, visual flow, rule of thirds, color harmony, negative space — as structural optimizations of the aesthetic functional rather than culturally contingent conventions, and assembles the interpretation into *VII.R21 — Visual Composition Principles*. Balance is the condition that the weighted center of visual mass coincides approximately with the frame's geometric center, making the composition stable under small rotations and translations of the viewing angle — invariance under perturbation. Visual flow is the gradient of decreasing aesthetic tension through the image: the eye follows the path from areas of high contrast (high 𝒜) toward areas of resolution (low 𝒜), the spatial analogue of musical resolution. The rule of thirds approximates golden-ratio subdivision of the frame, positioning key elements at the irrationality-maximizing locations that avoid both the stasis of dead center and the instability of the frame edge. Color harmony is invariance under perceptual grouping: harmonious palettes cohere into a single gestalt rather than fragmenting into competing elements. Cross-media validity — the same rules operating in oil painting, digital photography, and cinema — is identified as evidence for the structural rather than conventional character of these principles: a rule governing three technically distinct media reflects the invariant structure of the visual readout functor rather than any medium-specific tradition. The dissolution of direct versus indirect realism about composition follows from the general framework: compositional principles describe the morphism between image structure and readout functor, neither purely objective nor purely subjective.
