A structural claim earns its keep by organizing phenomena. Chapter 42 established the Beauty-as-Invariance identification on formal grounds; this chapter tests it against three domains with distinct admissible groups. In mathematics, Euler's identity e^{iπ} + 1 = 0 is a global section of the mathematical motif presheaf — it connects five fundamental constants through three operations and holds unchanged from every mathematical perspective. The Yoneda Lemma and the classification of finite simple groups are further instances: their aesthetic functional vanishes identically because they are invariant under the full scope of their respective admissible groups. Crucially, the chapter also distinguishes beauty of result from beauty of proof: the four-color theorem is invariant (its truth holds from every mathematical direction), but its original computer-assisted proof is not — it requires enumerating nearly two thousand cases, none of which illuminates the others. Result and proof are different motifs, and 𝒜 is evaluated separately for each. In nature, crystal lattices, bilateral facial symmetry, and logarithmic spirals each achieve near-zero or zero 𝒜-values with respect to their physical symmetry groups; the mechanism in each case is structural optimization, not human projection. In art, the Parthenon sits at 𝒜 ≈ 0 through proportional self-similarity across scales; Bach's fugal subjects achieve near-zero functional values by surviving transposition, inversion, and stretto without losing identity; Mondrian's late paintings reduce the visual field to its grid invariants. The central consolidating result is *VII.R18 — Beauty Across Domains*: in each domain the admissible group differs, but the motifs judged beautiful have vanishing or near-vanishing aesthetic functional, and the convergence is explained by a shared source — the kernel's own j-closed invariants projected through domain-specific readout functors.
