Environmental aesthetics is, genetically speaking, the first aesthetics: long before the first painting or composed melody, organisms registered the structural invariants of their environments — the symmetry of a potential mate, the regularity of a safe habitat, the dissonance of a disturbed landscape. This chapter treats natural beauty as grounded in genuine structural invariances — not projected by the observer — and shows that it tracks a real ecological property: the coherence and robustness of the ecosystem producing it. Individual natural objects exhibit specific invariance types: crystals hold under their space groups; flowers and snowflakes hold under discrete rotation; logarithmic spirals hold under scale-rotation; natural landscapes exhibit statistical invariance under scale change (fractal dimension in the 1.2–1.5 range). The chapter extends this from individual objects to systems: a healthy ecosystem exhibits high structural invariance through stable nutrient cycles (temporal periodic invariants), resilient populations (attractors in ecological phase space), balanced energy flows (smoothly composing morphisms in the ecological category), and redundant functional pathways (overlapping covers with sufficient gluing). A degraded ecosystem exhibits the opposite — a rise in 𝒜 across all these dimensions. The convergence of aesthetic judgment and ecological assessment — clear water is judged both more attractive and ecologically healthier; birdsong diversity registers as aesthetically rich and as an indicator of intact trophic relationships — is not coincidental. Both beauty and ecological health are instances of structural robustness: the system's capacity to maintain invariance under perturbation. The closing result *VII.R24 — Environmental Beauty* states this convergence and draws the structural bridge from aesthetics to ethics: if beauty tracks structural integrity, then the destruction of natural beauty is not merely an aesthetic loss but a loss of structural robustness whose further normative consequences are deferred to Part VII (Categorical Ethics).
