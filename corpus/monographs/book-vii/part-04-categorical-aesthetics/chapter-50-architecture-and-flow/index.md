Architecture occupies a distinctive position in the aesthetic landscape: it is the art form the body inhabits rather than observes. This chapter treats a building as a spatial category B whose objects are positions accessible to the body and whose morphisms are permissible movements between positions. The aesthetic quality of a building is then determined by the structural properties of this category: connectivity (every position reachable from every other), composability of morphisms (transitions between spaces feeling continuous rather than jarring), and symmetry (non-trivial automorphisms reducing informational complexity for the visitor). Flow is identified as traversal along low-tension morphisms — the spatial configuration channeling the body along paths that feel inevitable without signage. Good circulation is a commutative diagram in B; poor circulation is non-commutative, leaving the visitor disoriented by path-dependent spatial experiences. Sacred architecture — temples, cathedrals, mosques — is analyzed as the most sustained attempt to maximize resonance by aligning built proportions with kernel invariants: axial symmetry provides non-trivial automorphisms; proportional systems create scale self-similarity analogous to fractal aesthetics; threshold sequences (portico, narthex, sanctuary) constitute a filtration mirroring the enrichment hierarchy E_0 → E_3. The body is identified as the measure of architectural space: its proprioceptive expectations define the natural scale of resonance, and the sublime arises precisely when architectural scale exceeds those expectations. The consolidating result is *VII.R22 — Architecture as Spatial Aesthetics*, which identifies three structural consequences: architecture is the most fully embodied art; flow is aesthetic composability of morphisms; every building is a boundary archetype — an interface separating and connecting interior and exterior.
