The previous two chapters treated beauty and elegance as properties of individual motifs. This chapter lifts the analysis to collections and asks what structural patterns persist across an artist's body of work, a historical period, or a genre. The answer is given in categorical terms. An artist can be modeled as a readout functor R : Kernel → Medium; the artist's *style* is the collection of structural features preserved by R across all kernel inputs — the invariant content of the functor. A natural transformation η : R₁ ⇒ R₂ between two such functors is a systematic translation of outputs preserving compositional structure; if η is invertible, R₁ and R₂ are style-equivalent. A *genre* is then an equivalence class of works under style-preserving natural transformations, with genre boundaries naturally fuzzy where only partial natural transformations exist. Period styles — Baroque, Modernist — are natural transformation classes sharing a common structural invariant across domain-specific functors: Baroque functors preserve contrast and elaboration; Modernist functors optimize for maximal elegance. A *recurring motif* — the hero's journey, the lament, the labyrinth — is an archetypal shadow: the projection of a j-closed kernel invariant through different readout functors across cultures and historical periods. The consolidating result is *VII.R19 — Style as Categorical Invariant*, which places motif, style, genre, and recurring motif in a four-level hierarchy that emerges from the categorical structure of S_D rather than being imposed from outside.
