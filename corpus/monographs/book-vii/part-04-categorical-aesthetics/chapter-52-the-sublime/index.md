Beauty has been characterized throughout Part IV by the successful registration of invariance: the observer detects the motif, the readout functor delivers a coherent image, and the phenomenological result is some form of recognition. This chapter treats the limit case where registration fails not because the motif lacks invariance but because it has too much — because the kernel invariant exceeds the observer's finite processing capacity. The central definition is *VII.D50 — The Sublime as Comprehension Boundary*: a kernel invariant σ is sublime for observer C when dim(Reg_D(σ)) > κ(C), where κ(C) is the observer's finite processing capacity. The sublimity ratio s_C(σ) = dim(Reg_D(σ))/κ(C) measures the degree of excess; beauty corresponds to s_C ≤ 1, the sublime to s_C > 1. The comprehension boundary ∂_κ(C) is the set of kernel invariants at the edge of processability. The resulting remark *VII.R23 — Kant's Sublime in Categorical Terms* recovers Kant's two-fold distinction: the mathematical sublime occurs when the magnitude of the invariant overflows the readout's domain — the observer grasps local sections but cannot glue them into a global section, producing the characteristic vertigo of incompleteness; the dynamical sublime occurs when the invariant's intensity threatens the observer's structural integrity — the carrier's self-boundary is perturbed but not collapsed, and the observer registers its own resilience. Both modes produce elevation rather than mere confusion because the observer reads out not only the invariant but the limit of its own readout — a self-referential event structurally analogous to E_3 enrichment. The sublime is also identified as a register-boundary phenomenon: it begins in the diagrammatic sector S_D but crosses into the commitment sector S_C, because encountering the limit of one's own comprehension is an existential self-recognition, not merely a pattern-recognition event.
