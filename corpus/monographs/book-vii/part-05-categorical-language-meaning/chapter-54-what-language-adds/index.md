Language is not a neutral medium of representation. Part IV established that aesthetic engagement is pre-symbolic: the kernel's structure is felt before it is articulated. Part V asks what happens when a system acquires the capacity for symbolic representation. The categorical answer is that language is a readout functor—a projection of subsymbolic content into an expressive medium that has its own structure—and the medium's structure adds specific capacities that transform the E₃ observer's cognitive architecture. Three additions are identified: temporalization, the capacity to refer to states and events at temporal positions other than the speaker's present; mortality awareness, the reflexive application of temporal displacement to one's own existence; and self-reference, the capacity to name one's own states, frames, and operations. None of these is available to the subsymbolic layer alone.

## What this chapter contributes

- **Definitions / Axioms:** *VII.D51 — Language Adds Temporalization* (τ-effective). Temporalization is formalized as a family of operators T_past, T_present, T_future : Prop → Prop acting on the category of propositions, enabling the linguistic system to construct and communicate contents located at temporal positions other than the speaker's present.
- **Key results:** none in formal-derivation form; this chapter establishes the three structural additions of language (temporalization, mortality awareness, self-reference) and their four structural costs (ambiguity, drift, misrepresentation, register conflation), and lays out the plan for the remaining eleven chapters of Part V.
- **Dependencies:** Part IV account of pre-symbolic resonance; readout functor framework; four-register architecture (Reg_E, Reg_P, Reg_D, Reg_C); Gödel avoidance mechanisms.
