When language serves not a single observer but an entire community, it becomes a shared readout infrastructure that must satisfy a mutual calibration condition: for any pair of observers i, j and any pattern m in their shared domain, δ(ℓ_i(m), ℓ_j(m)) ≤ ε, where ε is the community's tolerance threshold. This formalization of public language—as a pair (Σ, {ℓ_i}_{i∈I}) of a shared symbol alphabet and a calibrated family of readout morphisms—has three immediate structural consequences. Publicity is a constraint on symbolic labels, not on subsymbolic patterns: an observer may harbour patterns that no label in Σ covers, but cannot use labels idiosyncratically. Natural language tolerance is broad; institutional language progressively narrows it. Legal language is the limit case: the diagrammatic register Reg_D pressed toward ε = 0. Contracts enumerate conditions and remedies whose sections must glue without gaps; statutes carry interpretation clauses that are explicit calibration instructions; constitutions function as readout functors on readout functors—second-order calibrations governing the interpretive practices of the legal system itself. Judicial interpretation is structurally inevitable, not a sign of drafting failure: natural language is finitely generated while the subsymbolic presheaf is not, so by the pigeonhole principle alone there will always be patterns that no available label covers uniquely. The precision–expressiveness trade-off is established as a structural proposition: expressive range Exp(ℓ) ∼ ε⁻¹; as tolerance approaches zero, the symbol carries a single fixed meaning and loses its penumbra of connotation, metaphor, and pragmatic implication. Wittgenstein's private language argument is reframed precisely: what it refutes is private symbols, not private patterns; the subsymbolic presheaf ℳ_sub is label-independent and is not constrained by the calibration condition.
