Language does not arrive from outside the cognitive system and impose itself on a pre-existing substrate. It arises when the subsymbolic communication system enriches itself to the point where it can encode its own patterns symbolically and transport them across contexts. This chapter addresses the genetic—structural, not historical—question of how this happens by locating language within the enrichment hierarchy E₀ → E₁ → E₂ → E₃. Subsymbolic communication operates at E₀ (pattern detection) and E₁ (structured signal exchange). Language is the E₂ enrichment: the system acquires a naming functor N : ℳ_sub → Sym that maps subsymbolic patterns to symbolic representatives, a compositional operation ⊗ on Sym, and a cross-context transport condition ensuring that N(m) communicated to a perspective V determines a pattern m' satisfying m' ≃ m|_{U ∩ V} on the overlap. These three conditions—symbolic encoding, compositionality, and transport—are proved to constitute the E₂ predicate. Naming is shown to be lossy: the symbolic representative is structurally faithful on the overlap but does not exhaust the original pattern. Transport is approximate for the same structural reason. The hierarchy terminates at E₃ (self-modelling language), with no E₄ by the Saturation Theorem, which has the linguistic consequence that there is no linguistic level beyond self-reflective language.
