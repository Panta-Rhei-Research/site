If relations are primary and τ is the ontic substrate, then existence means being an admissible construction within τ. This chapter makes that criterion precise. The Internal Set Ontology (*VII.D25*) defines ontic standing through NF-addressability: an entity exists within τ if and only if it admits a finite canonical address in the kernel's address space, and that address is unique. The boundedness condition (ISO2) follows directly: τ's internal ontology is intrinsically finitistic. The Ontic/Virtual Distinction (*VII.D26*) partitions the mathematical universe: ontic entities are internal constructions with NF addresses; virtual entities are definable within a meta-theoretic framework such as ZFC but lack any NF address. Actual infinities, proper classes, and non-constructive choice functions are virtual — legitimate for reasoning about τ but not part of τ's ground floor. The ZFC-as-VM thesis (*VII.R05*, from Book III) supplies the mechanism: orthodox set theory runs as a virtual machine on τ-hardware, preserving all classical theorems within the emulation layer while removing their claim to ontic status. Wigner's puzzle about the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics dissolves: physics and mathematics are two projections of the same kernel.

## What this chapter contributes

- **Definitions / Axioms:** *VII.D25 — Internal Set Ontology* (τ-effective): three conditions ISO1–ISO3 identifying existence with NF-addressability, boundedness, and uniqueness. *VII.D26 — Ontic/Virtual Distinction* (τ-effective): OV1–OV2 partition constructions into those with and without NF addresses.
- **Key results:** *VII.R05 — ZFC-as-VM Philosophical Import*: four-point remark developing the virtual-machine reading of orthodox set theory; explains independence phenomena and demystifies mathematical effectiveness.
- **Notation introduced:** NF address (normal-form address); ontic / virtual as technical terms.
