Solipsism cannot be refuted by argument — any argument presupposes an audience — and cannot be refuted by evidence — any evidence can be reinterpreted as a construction of the self. But it can be dissolved: shown to arise from a presupposition that relational ontology removes. The Three-Layer Solipsism Resolution definition (*VII.D39*) addresses three aspects of the solipsistic challenge. At the ontic layer (SR1): in τ there is one structure with a unique terminal point ω, so "only my self exists" read as "there is only one structure" is trivially true — but monism with internal distinctions is not solipsism, since the self is one position among many, with no ontological privilege relative to ω. More precisely, a relational position cannot be the only position: it requires other positions to have a morphism profile at all, so the solipsistic hypothesis is self-undermining by the Yoneda principle. At the epistemic layer (SR2): an E₃ observer modeling its own modeling necessarily encounters irreducible uncertainty about whether there are other modelers — the very capacity for self-modeling generates the question of other minds as a structural byproduct, constituting an epistemic witness without being a proof. At the Bayesian layer (SR3): the Bayesian Exclusion Theorem (*VII.T15*) proves that under finite computational resources C and observed complexity K ≫ C, the posterior probability of solipsism satisfies P(H_S | E) ≤ (C/K) · P(H_S) ≪ P(H_M | E), suppressing solipsism's posterior by the factor C/K regardless of the prior. The chapter closes by reinterpreting the Cartesian cogito: "I think, therefore I am" becomes "this relational position is a modeling position" — a structural fact, not a claim about a substance.
