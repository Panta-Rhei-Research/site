When an agent inhabits multiple social spheres — professional, intimate, civic, online — the social sheaf requires compatible local sections on every overlap. The sheaf axiom is strict: if two local sections disagree on their intersection, no global section can extend both. This chapter makes fragmentation and role conflict the direct structural consequences of that axiom. When the number and incompatibility of spheres exceed an agent's capacity to perform coherence work, three structural exits remain: modify at least one local section to restore compatibility, withdraw from one or more spheres, or maintain incompatible local selves at the cost of coherence. The Fragmentation Proposition (*VII.P24 — τ-effective*) formalizes this trichotomy. Information overload is then analysed as a specific form of cover failure amplified by modern networked environments: persistence (interactions become permanent records), audience collapse (multiple spheres observe the same utterance), acceleration, and monetized attention all increase the patch count while degrading gluing capacity. The structural diagnosis throughout is not that individuals are weak but that the cover has grown faster than the available coherence mechanisms. The chapter closes with four categories of structural mitigation — boundary design, institutional clarity, local spheres, and media hygiene — each targeting a different source of the failure.
