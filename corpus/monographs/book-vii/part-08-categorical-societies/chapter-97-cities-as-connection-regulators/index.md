Once a social carrier set far exceeds the Dunbar threshold, the problem of coherence cannot be solved by thick interpersonal recognition. The city's structural achievement is not to eliminate this problem but to regulate around it: holding connection density in a regime where coordination remains feasible without requiring that every agent recognize every other. Regulation, not maximization, is the operative concept. This chapter frames the characteristic features of urban life — anonymity, role-based formality, neighbourhood sub-spheres, weak ties, and cosmopolitan coexistence — as structural consequences of thin overlaps and mediated gluing. Urban infrastructure (transport, zoning, communication systems) defines the effective morphism network on Ω_soc: it determines which encounters are likely, which overlaps exist, and what covering families the Grothendieck topology actually provides. Anonymity emerges as a structural achievement rather than a deficiency, freeing agents from the surveillance that dense recognition entails. Cosmopolitan tolerance is reframed as a practical solution to gluing under plural value systems: it supplies compatibility conditions on overlaps when deeper agreement is unavailable. The Urban Topology remark (*VII.R36 — τ-effective*) summarizes the morphism-network picture.
