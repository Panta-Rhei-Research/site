Political philosophy asks how collective order emerges from multiplicity of agents with divergent interests. This chapter provides the categorical account, drawing on the social ontology and the sheaf framework developed throughout Part VIII. Power is formalised as an asymmetric morphism P: A → B in the social category — agent A's actions constrain B's possibilities more than B's constrain A's — with scope limited to an open set U and modality distinguishing coercive, economic, ideological, and institutional forms (*VII.D80 — conjectural*). Sovereignty is the terminal object in the lattice of political authorities within a domain. Legitimacy, the normative core of the chapter, is defined as recognition coherence: a power relation is legitimate if and only if the governed entities' local recognition sections glue to a global section on U, that gluing is grounded in justification that survives the universalisability test of the CI sheaf, and the exercise of authority factors through the dignity reflector (*VII.P25 — conjectural*). Weber's typology — traditional, charismatic, rational-legal, and democratic — is reformulated in terms of the covering morphisms that generate the recognition gluing in each case. A legitimacy crisis is a gluing failure: local recognition sections cease to be compatible on overlaps. Justice is characterised structurally as the condition under which arrangements satisfy the dignity constraint, the CI sheaf condition, and the fairness protocols; Rawls's original position is reinterpreted as a label-independence condition on just principles. The chapter carries a conjectural scope label, reflecting that the claim of adequacy of these formulations to the full complexity of political life goes beyond what structural argument alone can establish.

## What this chapter contributes

- **Definitions / Axioms:** *VII.D80 — Power as Morphism Structure* (conjectural). An asymmetric morphism P: A → B with specified scope U and four modalities; domination is power exercised without legitimating grounds and without effective recourse.
- **Key results:** *VII.P25 — Legitimacy as Recognition Coherence* (conjectural): a power relation is legitimate iff claim, recognition, coherence (gluing of recognition sections), justification (CI universalisability), and dignity preservation jointly hold.
- **Dependencies:** *VII.D76* (social ontology), *VII.D79* (nine global spheres), dignity foundation, CI sheaf, fairness protocols, from Chapters 94–103 and Part VII.
