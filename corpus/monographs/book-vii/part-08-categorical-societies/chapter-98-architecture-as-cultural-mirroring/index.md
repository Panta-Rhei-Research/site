Urban infrastructure determines which morphisms exist on Ω_soc; architecture determines the fine-grained conditions under which those morphisms operate. This chapter treats built form not as decoration but as a principal mediator of social topology: its encounter rates, legibility, access rules, boundary thickness, and transition gradients shape which recognition morphisms are possible and which social facts can be sustained. The Architecture as Cultural Readout remark (*VII.R37 — τ-effective*) formalizes this as a readout functor from social structure to physical form — bidirectional, because buildings encode existing social structure while constraining future social possibilities. Power's architectural expression is analysed through the recurring mechanisms of centralized visibility, constrained pathways, and checkpoint dependence: each of these modifies the morphism network in ways that can support or undermine reciprocal recognition, making them subject to the dignity constraint. Sacred and profane spatial distinctions are reinterpreted structurally as boundary invariants: temples, courts, and civic halls employ the same architectural grammar — threshold marking, orientation, controlled atmosphere — to stabilize a special register of social facts. The temporal dimension is addressed through the observation that built form persists longer than individual agents, giving architecture a distinctive role in transmitting social norms across generations without relying on personal recall.
