Recognition morphisms are not free. Each active morphism ρ_{i→j} requires agent i to maintain a model of agent j that tracks identity-invariants across time and context, consuming cognitive and temporal resources that are finite. This chapter takes that constraint seriously and derives its structural consequence: any agent with capacity for at most K deep recognition morphisms can participate in all-to-all thick recognition with at most K + 1 others. Beyond that threshold, gluing of the social sheaf requires institutional mediation — roles, rules, and procedures — rather than direct interpersonal familiarity. The empirical Dunbar number (K ≈ 150) enters as input to the framework, not as a derived constant; what the framework supplies is the structural explanation of why such a threshold produces a qualitative phase transition in how social coherence is achieved. Rural and village forms illustrate the below-threshold regime: repeated encounters, thick overlapping roles (neighbour, coworker, kin), clear membership, and low dependence on formal machinery. The classical Gemeinschaft/Gesellschaft contrast is recast not as a cultural preference but as a difference in gluing mechanism.
