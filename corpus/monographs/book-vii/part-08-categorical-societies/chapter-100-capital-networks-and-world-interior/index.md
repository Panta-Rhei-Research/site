Economic life is not a separate domain adjacent to social structure; it operates through the same recognition graph that constitutes Ω_soc. This chapter builds on that continuity by defining capital as a network phenomenon: the Capital Network (*VII.D78 — τ-effective*) is a weighted directed graph over the social base space where capital for any agent is the capacity to mobilize resources through the edges incident to that node — a function of centrality, institutional access, and convertible assets. Capital decomposes into partially convertible components (financial, institutional, cultural, social), and the conversion relations between them generate the structural dynamics the chapter analyses. Hub formation follows from feedback between visibility, access, and reputation: each advantage attracts more of the same, producing heavy-tailed distributions without requiring any single moral or psychological explanation. Sloterdijk's world interior of capital is interpreted as a claim about enclosure: capitalism constructs a vast interior by expanding the commensurability regime — more goods become tradable, more relationships become contractual, more environments become infrastructurally integrated. The dignity constraint of *VII.D78* marks the categorical limit: not every relation should be mediated by exchange, because some identity-invariants are not commensurable. Poverty, in this framework, is peripheral position with few, weak, and unreliable edges — an access topology problem as much as an income problem.
