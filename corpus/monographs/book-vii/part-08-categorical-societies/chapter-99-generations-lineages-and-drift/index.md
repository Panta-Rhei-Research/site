Social facts are sheaf sections that must survive not only spatial overlap but also time. This chapter adds the temporal axis to the Part VIII framework by modelling generational transmission as the iterated composition of sheaf-section operators: each generation applies a transmission operator 𝒢_t to the norm-space it inherits, producing a successor section that may differ from the original. The monodromy vocabulary from earlier in Book VII enters here as cultural drift: when a practice is transported through a temporal loop and returns with altered meaning, the holonomy of that loop measures the accumulated semantic shift. Drift is not random; it concentrates where contexts change quickly, where attention is scarce, and where institutional carriers weaken. Lineages are the structures that resist this erosion — they are temporally persistent carrier structures (code streams) consisting of evolving community open sets and transmission operators, functioning analogously to genetic lineages in Book VI: the cultural code is replicated with variation, and selection operates on the transmitted variants. The Cultural Lineage as Code Stream remark (*VII.R38 — τ-effective*) makes this structural parallel explicit. Three examples — craft traditions, religious lineages, and scientific disciplines — illustrate how drift operates and when it can produce rapid collapse or stable long-run transmission.
