The Part VIII framework has moved from individual agents with dignity to spheres, communities, cities, temporal lineages, and economic networks. This chapter completes the scalar picture with a systematic classification of nine structural levels at which social life organises itself: self/body (n = 1), intimate pair (n = 2), family/household (n ≈ 3–10), friendship circle (n ≈ 10–50), community/village (n ≈ 150, the Dunbar sphere), city/region (n ≈ 10³–10⁷), nation/state (n ≈ 10⁶–10⁹), civilisation/culture zone (n ≈ 10⁸–10⁹), and planetary/species-wide (n ≈ 8 × 10⁹). The classification is not arbitrary: each level is characterised by its recognition density, boundary type (affective at lower spheres, institutional at higher ones), and coordination mode. Four structural patterns cut across the taxonomy — recognition density decreasing with scale, boundary type shifting, coordination mode shifting, and dignity tracking weakening as recognition thins. The Nine Global Spheres Classification (*VII.D79 — τ-effective*) formalises this. The central structural problem that the classification reveals is scale mismatch: a problem whose effects propagate at sphere level k but whose institutional apparatus operates at level j < k generates a section on a large open set that available covers of smaller open sets cannot glue. Climate, pandemics, technology governance, and financial contagion are analysed as concrete instances. Political backlash as re-scaling — retreating to a lower sphere where recognition density supports legitimacy — is identified as a structural response with the predictable cost of abandoning coordination at the mismatched higher level.

## What this chapter contributes

- **Definitions / Axioms:** *VII.D79 — Nine Global Spheres Classification* (τ-effective). Nine levels from self to planetary, each characterised by carrier type, recognition topology, and characteristic membership scale.
- **Key results:** none in formal-derivation form; this chapter establishes the mismatch principle (problems at level k require governance at level k, not at level j < k) and surveys five approaches to global coordination that do not require a planetary sovereign.
- **Dependencies:** *VII.D76* (social ontology), *VII.D77* (sphere as shared topos), *VII.P23* (Dunbar limit), *VII.R36* (urban topology), *VII.P24* (fragmentation), from Chapters 94–101.
