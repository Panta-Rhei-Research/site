Experience is irreducibly temporal; we perceive durations, not instants. The preceding chapters developed knowledge, justification, and perception in largely atemporal terms. This chapter introduces the temporal cover: the category Temp whose objects are finite intervals of phenomenological duration and whose morphisms are inclusions. The Temporal Experience Structure definition formalizes Husserl's three moments — retention (s|_{Tᵢ⁻}, the overlap with the immediately preceding context), primal impression (s|_{Tᵢ⁰}, the present core), and protention (s|_{Tᵢ⁺}, the overlap with the succeeding context) — as three restrictions of a single global section s rather than three separate mental acts. Temporal synthesis — experienced duration — is the gluing condition: the protention of one context must equal the retention of the next on their shared overlap. The chapter also sharply distinguishes retention from memory: retention is a restriction within the current cover; memory is reconstruction of a section over a different, past cover. The Husserl-τ Correspondence remark confirms the match at four structural points: non-independence of moments, continuous modification via restriction maps, Husserl's double intentionality as local versus longitudinal gluing conditions, and pre-reflective temporality as constitutive section-existence rather than a higher-order act.

## What this chapter contributes

- **Definitions / Axioms:** *VII.D45 — Temporal Experience Structure* (τ-effective). Retention, primal impression, and protention are the restrictions of a global experiential section to the backward overlap, present core, and forward overlap of each temporal context; temporally unified experience is a global section satisfying the gluing condition on all consecutive overlaps.
- **Key results:** *VII.R13 — Husserl–τ Correspondence*: the sheaf formalization matches Husserl's phenomenological analysis at four structural points (non-independence, continuous modification, double intentionality, pre-reflective temporality), confirming the correspondence as structural rather than merely analogical.
- **Dependencies:** *VII.D44* (Perception Functor, Chapter 35); Chapter 27 (worlds-topos presheaf framework); ontological account of time (causation-space-time chapter in Part II).
