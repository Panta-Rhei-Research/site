The Western philosophical tradition has treated ontology and epistemology as distinct disciplines since Aristotle; the gap between them has generated three centuries of philosophical difficulty from Cartesian doubt through Kantian agnosticism to analytic skepticism. This chapter argues that the gap is not a genuine structural feature but an artefact of substance ontology — the assumption that the knowing subject and the known object are different kinds of thing. In relational ontology both are nodes in the same web, characterized entirely by their morphism profiles. The "gap" is a morphism path in the web; knowing is traversal. The Ontology-Epistemology Collapse Theorem establishes a formal adjunction Φ : Kn_C ⇄ Ont : Ψ between the category of knowledge-sections constructible by an E₃ observer C and the category of admissible ontological constructions in τ. Φ sends a section to the sub-pattern it covers; Ψ sends a construction to the maximal section an observer can build over contexts including its NF address. Under sufficient resources — when C's cover reaches all of τ's address space — the adjunction becomes a categorical equivalence Kn_C ≃ Ont. The gap is not zero for finite observers; it is computable as the uncovered region of the address space, and reducible by cover enlargement. Three traditional positions are diagnosed: skepticism mistakes a finite cover deficit for a metaphysical abyss; idealism mistakes the observer's role in constructing sections for a role in constructing reality; naïve realism fails to acknowledge that all access is register-mediated, though the mediation is faithful.
