The preceding chapters deferred a foundational question: what determines which observation contexts are available to an E₃ observer? The answer is the body. An observer is not a disembodied point of view; it is a materially instantiated carrier node whose sensorimotor repertoire determines which contexts it can occupy and which overlaps it can check. The phenomenological distinction between Leib (body as lived from the inside, the medium of felt agency) and Körper (body as observed from outside, an object among objects) is reformulated as a register distinction rather than a metaphysical dualism: Körper is the readout functor Reg_E applied to the carrier C; Leib is Reg_C applied to the same carrier. One carrier, two register projections. The body is formalized as the carrier's self-referential boundary ∂C ↪ C — the substructure of morphisms connecting C's internal self-model to its external environment — which has the topology of the lemniscate archetype S¹ ∨ S¹: two loops meeting at a crossing point that is the lived present, where interiority touches exteriority without collapsing into it. The phantom limb is diagnosed as a register mismatch: the commitment register retains boundary morphisms for the amputated limb while the empirical register no longer has corresponding sensory contexts. No body, no readout; no readout, no perception, knowledge, or experience. Embodiment is a constitutive condition, not an accidental feature.
