In normal operation, a *Reg_D*-theorem constrains the available *Reg_C*-stances: if the framework proves X structurally necessary, then any commitment that denies X is incoherent. At ω this normal relationship breaks down — not because the question is ill-formed or the answer unknown, but because the boundary collapse (Chapter 125) delivers no *Reg_D*-certified content at all. The D→C bridge functor B_{D→C} is structurally intact at ω; its input set is simply empty. Chapter 126 draws the precise consequence: no *Reg_D*-argument can force theism ("ω is inhabited"), atheism ("ω is empty"), or agnosticism ("I withhold judgment") — all three are *Reg_C*-stances, and the No Forced Stance theorem applies symmetrically to each. The landscape the framework delivers has definite shape — the enrichment ladder converges, the Logos sector exists, ι_τ threads through all seven books — but shape is *Reg_D*-content, shareable and certifiable. The stance within that shaped landscape is *Reg_C*-content: personal, performative, non-transferable. This openness is not a concession; it is the structural origin of freedom of conscience.
