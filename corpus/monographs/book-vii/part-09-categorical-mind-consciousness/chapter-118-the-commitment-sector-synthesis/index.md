The commitment sector S_C closes here. Part IX opened with a question embedded in the formula of the commitment register Reg_C — "What am I willing to live as true?" — and identified the analysis required by that formula's first word: what is the I that does the willing? Thirteen chapters have built a structural answer. This synthesis chapter catalogues the deliverables in order — mind as internal topos, narrative identity, consciousness as global section, intentionality, qualia, self-recognition, free will, personal identity, artificial mind criteria, the Metzinger comparison, emotions, and cognitive extension — and then examines what the arc as a whole delivers. The sector's currency is stance-stability: not observational adequacy (Reg_E), practical normativity (Reg_P), or formal provability (Reg_D), but the capacity of an agent to sustain a proposition as an organising centre of its life under metacognitive scrutiny and emotional pressure. A commitment is genuinely stable when it coheres with the agent's narrative identity (Chapter 107), survives register-crossing pressure (Chapter 116), and remains endorsed by the E₃ self-recognition loop (Chapter 111). The chapter's centrepiece is the D–C interface — the structural locus where Reg_D and Reg_C meet. The two registers use different currencies (proof-validity versus stance-stability) and are independent in general: one can prove things one does not commit to, and commit to things one cannot prove. But there are moments of convergence where what survives diagrammatic certification is the same content that survives the test of lived experience. That convergence is the threshold of the Logos sector S_L, opened in Part X.
