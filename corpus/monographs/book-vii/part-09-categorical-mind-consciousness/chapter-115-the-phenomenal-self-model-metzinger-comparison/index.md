This chapter positions the categorical account of mind against the most developed rival phenomenological framework: Thomas Metzinger's phenomenal self-model (PSM) theory. Metzinger's two central claims — the self is a maintained representational model, and that model is transparent (ordinarily experienced as unmediated reality rather than as a construction) — align closely with what the τ framework delivers from the internal-topos side. The structural parallels are mapped in detail: Metzinger's PSM corresponds to the story functor's output 𝒮 in 𝓜; his system-model parallels the mind-topos itself; transparency is explained in both accounts by the internal/external distinction; and both frameworks support graded self-modelling complexity across species. The differences, however, are consequential. Metzinger works within cognitive neuroscience and assumes a biological substrate; the categorical account is substrate-independent by construction (Definition VII.D82). Metzinger's representationalism asks how a brain-constructed model hooks onto its target; the relational-ontology account dissolves that question by treating target and representation as jointly constituted by morphism structure. Most importantly, Metzinger's no-self thesis — "no such things as selves exist in the world" — is disambiguated: substance-denial is accepted (no unchanging self-substance), but structure-denial is rejected (the self-object 𝒮 is a genuine structural feature of 𝓜, maintained and revised over time). The ego tunnel is reformulated as a restricted global section of 𝓜, centred on 𝒮 and transparent in Metzinger's sense, bridging both frameworks.
