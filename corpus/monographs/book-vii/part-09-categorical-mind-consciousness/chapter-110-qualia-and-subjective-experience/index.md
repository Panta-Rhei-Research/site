What is it like to see red? The question is not about the wavelength or the neural response but about qualitative character — the felt redness of the experience. This chapter models a quale as an internal morphism q : P → Q in the mind-topos, where P represents the stimulus and bodily condition as registered by the carrier's sensory and interoceptive systems, and Q is the corresponding phenomenal presentation. The morphism q is the qualitative transformation: how "what is happening" becomes "what it is like." Crucially, this does not posit a non-physical ingredient; it treats qualitative character as structured internal transformation embedded in the inferential and discriminative economy of 𝓜. Privacy follows in a principled way: an external observer can describe the role of q in the carrier's internal economy (inputs, outputs, downstream effects) without thereby instantiating q in its own topos. Shared biology supports partial vocabulary coordination via approximate structural isomorphism, but the description/instantiation gap is real. The chapter's central remark — the Hard Problem reformulated as a layer question between E₂ structural description and E₃ instantiation — locates the residual difficulty precisely without claiming to dissolve it. The entire chapter carries the conjectural scope label, and the closing section explicitly states three things the framework does not resolve: the existence question, the distribution question, and the comparison question.
