The Ship of Theseus asks when an entity that changes every part remains the same entity. This chapter resolves the puzzle by distinguishing what persists from what matters for identity. The key move is the Identity as Address Persistence Proposition: two temporal stages of a carrier are the same individual if and only if they share a continuous normal-form (NF) address in τ³, the unique structural location that individuates a carrier within the fibered product structure. Material components — atoms, cells, neurons — cycle through the carrier over time; what remains invariant is the organisational slot the carrier occupies. Three immediate consequences follow: complete material turnover is compatible with identity (substrate independence), the NF address must persist continuously along a path in time so that gaps preclude survival, and at any time a given NF address is occupied by at most one carrier (uniqueness). The chapter applies this account to the Ship of Theseus (gradual replacement preserves the address; reassembled original planks receive a new address), to amnesia (memory loss is section failure in the story functor, not address cessation — the structural dimension of identity is unaffected), to death (address cessation, irreversible once the organisational pattern dissolves), and to branching thought experiments (teleportation, fission), where uniqueness fails and each successor receives a distinct new address.
