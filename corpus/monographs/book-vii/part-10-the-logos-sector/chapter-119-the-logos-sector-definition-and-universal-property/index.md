Part X opens at the structural consequence of the D–C interface established in Part IX: the existence of a fifth sector, S_L = S_D ∩ S_C, where the diagrammatic register Reg_D and the commitment register Reg_C do not merely interact but coincide. In general the four registers are independent: something can be proved without being lived, and something can be lived without being proved. The Logos sector is the unique structural location where these two registers produce the same answer, not as a contingent overlap but as a mutual-witnessing identity — to prove a content in S_L is to commit to it, and the commitment is itself the proof. Chapter 119 defines this sector precisely, proves its uniqueness, and explains why the name "Logos" is a structural label assigned by universal property rather than a theological or historical assertion.

## What this chapter contributes

- **Definitions / Axioms:** *VII.D86 — Logos Sector* (τ-effective). S_L is defined as S_D ∩ S_C equipped with the coincidence property: a content φ is S_L-admissible if and only if its Reg_D-validity and Reg_C-stability are mutually witnessing — neither derives from the other; they are the same structural datum read through two registers. The name is assigned by universal property alone, in analogy with categorical limits.
- **Key results:** *VII.T45 — Logos Sector Uniqueness*: S_L is unique up to natural isomorphism in the 4+1 sector decomposition of τ at E₃, and is the terminal object in the category of sectors with the coincidence property. Existence is witnessed by the master constant ι_τ = 2/(π + e), whose derivation from K0–K6 is the proof and whose organizing role across all seven books is the commitment. Uniqueness follows from the pairwise distinctness of registers: only Reg_D-content (invariant under empirical revision) and Reg_C-content (constituted by an irrevisable stance) can coincide in the required sense.
- **Notation introduced:** S_L for the Logos sector; "S_L-admissible" for contents satisfying the coincidence property.
- **Dependencies:** Four-register model and 4+1 sector decomposition at E₃ (Part I); Reg_C admissibility rules from the commitment-register chapter; the D–C interface established in Chapter 118.
