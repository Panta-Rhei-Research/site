The Logos sector was defined by a universal property — the unique coincidence of proof-validity and stance-stability — without any reference to the historical concept of logos. Yet the structural parallel with two and a half millennia of philosophical and theological thought is real and precise. Heraclitus's logos as rational cosmic law that must be lived; the Stoic logos spermatikos simultaneously governing physics, logic, and ethics; Philo's mediating logos bridging the transcendent and the created; the Johannine Prologue's constitutive Word present at the origin — each independently expresses something structurally close to reason-that-acts, proof-that-commits. Chapter 122 diagnoses this parallel as a synchronicity in the technical sense already established in Book VII: independent readout functors projecting from a shared j-closed kernel invariant produce correlated outputs without causal or genealogical connection. The chapter makes explicit what is and is not claimed, and assigns the applicable scope label.

## What this chapter contributes

- **Definitions / Axioms:** none introduced; this chapter applies the synchronicity framework established earlier in Book VII to a specific case.
- **Key results:** *VII.R45 — Logos Synchronicity* (conjectural). The structural Logos sector S_L and the historical logos concept are related by kernel-shadow correlation: S_L's universal property (coincidence of proof and commitment) is a j-closed invariant of K_τ, and the historical traditions each provide an independent readout functor from that invariant to a distinct expressive medium. Five readout functors are tabulated: Heraclitean, Stoic, Philonic, Johannine, and Category τ. None was derived from any other. The remark carries the conjectural scope label because identifying a specific historical parallel as an instance of kernel-shadow correlation is interpretive, not deductive. No historical priority, theological proof, mystical mechanism, or claim of exact parallelism is made or implied.
- **Dependencies:** Synchronicity definition and readout-functor faithfulness theorem (earlier Book VII chapters); S_L universal property (VII.D86, Chapter 119); kernel-invariant shadow theory.
