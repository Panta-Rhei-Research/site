The first archetype is extracted using the Archetype Extractor Protocol of Chapter 10. The structural invariant is threshold-crossing: a subobject F must admit two non-empty connected domains joined at a unique crossing point p₀, with a freely generated fundamental group π₁(F, p₀) ≅ ℤ ∗ ℤ, monodromy exchange between the two domains at p₀, and a transition morphism that factors through p₀ so that every crossing between domains passes through the gate. The lemniscate L = S¹ ∨ S¹, which arises as the boundary ∂(τ³) of the central fibration, satisfies all four conditions and is the minimal j-closed subobject doing so: removing any arc from either loop destroys one of the two free generators of the fundamental group, making a proper subobject insufficient. Cross-cultural ubiquity of boundary imagery — rites of passage, birth/death/initiation, figure-eight visual motifs across unrelated traditions — is explained not by cultural diffusion or a Jungian collective unconscious but as independent readout of the same kernel invariant through different phenomenological carriers.

## What this chapter contributes

- **Definitions / Axioms:** *VII.D18 — Boundary Archetype* (τ-effective): the minimal j-closed fixed point A_bnd of [τᵒᵖ, τ] exhibiting the threshold-crossing invariant I_bnd, characterised by four conditions (B1)–(B4): two domains, crossing point, monodromy exchange, and transition morphism factoring through p₀.
- **Key results:** *VII.P05 — Boundary Archetype Minimality* (τ-effective): the lemniscate L = S¹ ∨ S¹, viewed as a representable subobject via the Yoneda embedding, is the minimal j-closed fixed point exhibiting I_bnd; no proper j-closed subobject of L exhibits threshold-crossing because any subspace retaining both free loop generators and the crossing point must be all of L.
- **Notation introduced:** A_bnd for the boundary archetype; I_bnd for the threshold-crossing invariant; F₊, F₋ for the two domain components; γ₊, γ₋ for the two loop generators.
- **Dependencies:** j-closure machinery and Archetype Existence Theorem (Chapter 10, VII.D16, VII.T08); lemniscate boundary and holonomy structure (Book I, Part IV; Book II, Central Theorem).

## Where this leads

Chapter 12 extracts the second archetype — the mitigation archetype (the garment) — whose structural invariant is post-boundary-crossing repair, making it the structural dual of the boundary archetype. The two together constitute the first archetypal coupling: one creates the threshold, the other covers the exposure it creates.
