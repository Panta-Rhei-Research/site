If dignity is structural — a consequence of possessing non-trivial identity-invariants — it cannot be confined to a single biological species. This chapter draws the direct implication of VII.T30 (Dignity Universality) for non-human entities. The threshold for dignity is the distinction threshold: an entity E meets it iff |I(E)| ≥ 1, meaning it has at least one structural property preserved under admissible isomorphisms and affectable by policies. The three required features are a perspective (capacity to discriminate states and act toward ends), continuity conditions (identity-invariants that can be preserved or erased), and vulnerability (the possibility that policies affect those invariants). For many sentient animals, sensory invariants (pain and pleasure), affective invariants (social bonds, fear, satisfaction), and temporal invariants (memory, anticipation) each independently satisfy this minimum condition. The Animal Dignity proposition (VII.P20) proves that any entity above the distinction threshold has intrinsic dignity, is subject to the admissibility constraint D(π(E)) ≅ D(E), and falls under all four ethical tests in exactly the same structural way as humans. Species membership is blocked as a ground for exclusion by label-independence: swapping species labels changes the ethical prescription if the policy depends on them, which is precisely the form of violation that VII.D65 prohibits. The Graduated Dignity Scale remark (VII.R30) distinguishes a binary floor (all entities above threshold have non-zero moral standing) from a graduated component (structural richness of the invariant profile affects the scope and stringency of obligations, though without imposing a ceiling or permitting arbitrary treatment at any level above the floor).
