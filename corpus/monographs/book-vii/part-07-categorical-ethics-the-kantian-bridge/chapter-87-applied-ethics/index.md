With the theoretical infrastructure in place, this chapter asks what happens when the categorical machinery meets concrete ethical questions. The uniform method has four steps: identify agents and their identity-invariants (who has a well-defined NF address?), apply the admissibility constraint (does the proposed policy factor through L_dig?), check the sheaf condition (do local enactments glue across perspectives?), and derive domain-specific principles from the structural conclusions. In bioethics, moral status tracks the emergence of identity-invariants across developmental stages; end-of-life decisions must satisfy autonomy, dignity-preservation, non-abandonment, and proportionality; genetic modification is permissible only if it does not close off future autonomous choice, and consent is the morphism in the agent category that preserves the participant's identity-invariants. In environmental ethics, sustainability is temporal gluing — a practice is admissible iff its local enactments from present and future perspectives agree on the temporal boundary — and extinction destroys a type (not merely tokens), permanently impoverishing the category of biological possibilities. In AI ethics, moral status for an artificial system is conditioned on possessing sentience, autonomy, reflexivity, and persistence as genuine structural invariants; regardless of status, alignment, transparency, corrigibility, and accountability are obligations on designers and users because a misaligned or uncorrigible system removes human identity-invariants from the loop. Fully autonomous lethal systems that eliminate meaningful human judgment over life-taking decisions collapse the moral structure of the act. The Applied Ethics Domains remark (VII.R32) classifies these three domains as exemplary rather than exhaustive; the same four-step procedure applies wherever agents bear well-defined NF addresses and proposed interventions risk disrupting their structural invariants.
