Parts II and III have defined and instantiated the two base sectors. Part II (persistence, α-generator) instantiated in Archaea; Part III (agency, π-generator) instantiated in Bacteria. This closing chapter of Part III unifies both treatments with Woese's three-domain classification, delivers the central 2nd-edition correction about the nature of Eukarya, and prepares the structural transition to Parts IV–VI that develop the fiber-level sectors.

The Three-Domain Sector Taxonomy (VI.D35) reinterprets Woese's classification through the 4+1 sector template. Archaea ↔ Persistence sector (α-base): ether-linked membranes, chemolithotrophic metabolism, extremophile robustness, minimal genome change over ~3.5 Gyr, phylogenetic position nearest LUCA. Bacteria ↔ Agency sector (π-base): flagellar locomotion, chemotaxis, horizontal gene transfer, biofilm formation, planetary-scale metabolic diversification (including the Great Oxidation Event). The correspondence in both cases is an archetype assignment — it identifies the dominant and ancestral sector, not a bijective identification of every member. Individual archaea can exhibit agency (chemotaxis); individual bacteria can exhibit persistence (spore formation). The sector captures the typical and ancestral character.

The central 2nd-edition correction is stated explicitly through the Eukarya as Fiber-Enabled Regime remark (VI.R13). Eukarya is not a third sector parallel to Archaea and Bacteria; it is a carrier technology. The defining feature of eukaryotic cells is the possession of internal membrane architecture — nucleus, endomembrane system, mitochondria, chloroplasts — that internalizes the metabolic fiber T². This internalization enables eukaryotic lineages to run any of the five sectors: sessile protists (persistence), motile flagellates (agency), plants (source, γ-fiber), fungi (closure, η-fiber), animals (consumer, γ+η fiber pair). No prokaryotic domain spans all five. Eukarya is a platform, not a structural role. The biological mechanism of fiber acquisition is endosymbiosis: an α-proteobacterium engulfed by an archaeal host ~1.5–2.0 Gya provided mitochondria and oxidative phosphorylation capacity; a cyanobacterium engulfed ~1.0–1.5 Gya provided chloroplasts and source-sector access.

The tree of life is then decorated with sector labels. Each node v carries σ(v) ∈ {S_α, S_π, S_γ, S_η, S_(γ,η)}; edges along which σ changes are sector transitions. LUCA is assigned to S_α (reconstructed genome indicates anaerobic autotrophic chemolithotrophy matching modern methanogens). The first sector transition — the divergence of Bacteria from the Archaea–Eukarya lineage — is the shift from S_α to S_π. Horizontal gene transfer creates lateral cross-edges in the graph, explaining why the bacterial phylogeny is a network rather than a tree: the π-sector naturally produces network topology through informational spatial displacement. The most consequential lateral transfer is endosymbiosis itself, modeled as a merger edge: two nodes from different sectors collapsing into a single chimeric node that belongs to no sector but enables all. The Sector Assignment Table (VI.R12) maps major taxa — methanogens, halophiles, thermophiles, E. coli, cyanobacteria, Clostridium, land plants, fungi, animals, and cross-sector organisms such as mixotrophic protists, parasitic plants, and lichens — to their dominant generator, fiber access status, and key metabolic signature.
