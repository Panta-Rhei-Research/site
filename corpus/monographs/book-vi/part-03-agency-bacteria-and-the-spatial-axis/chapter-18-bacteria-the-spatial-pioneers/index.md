Chapter 17 defined the agency sector and identified the domain Bacteria as its biological archetype, placing the formal predicates — displacement, distinction maintenance, environmental coupling — in abstract terms. This chapter delivers the biology. The domain Bacteria instantiates spatial motility at every level of organization: individual cells move through flagellar rotation, pilus retraction, or gliding; populations coordinate movement through quorum sensing; lineages spread genetic innovations across species boundaries through horizontal gene transfer. Archaea persist by enduring; Bacteria persist by spreading.

The Bacteria as Agency Archetype remark (VI.R10) presents the three-column matching parallel to the persistence archetype (VI.R06): displacement is realized as flagellar swimming, twitching, or gliding (~90% of bacterial species are motile); distinction maintenance is realized as the peptidoglycan cell wall and plasma membrane remaining intact throughout locomotion; and environmental coupling is realized as chemotaxis, phototaxis, and magnetotaxis. The structural contrast with Archaea is sharpened: ester-linked membrane lipids (dynamic, metabolically flexible, less thermally stable than archaeal ether lipids) and peptidoglycan cell walls reflect the agency archetype's trade-off of thermodynamic resilience for metabolic plasticity.

The Motility Catalog (VI.R11) covers the three principal mechanisms in physical detail. The bacterial flagellar motor — a ~45 nm rotary engine of ~25 proteins, powered by proton-motive force at nearly 100% efficiency, rotating at 100–300 Hz — is the paradigmatic π-orbit engine: each revolution advances the cell spatially, the membrane remains intact, and CheY-P modulates motor direction in response to the chemotactic gradient. Type IV pili generate retraction forces of 100–180 pN (among the strongest known molecular motors) for surface twitching. Gliding, exemplified by Myxococcus xanthus, employs focal adhesion complexes that remain stationary relative to the substrate while the cell body moves over them. The convergent evolution of all three mechanisms from unrelated molecular machinery illustrates the sector's nature as a categorical constraint, not a mechanical one: π-orbit motility can be realized by any physical implementation that satisfies the three formal conditions.

Biofilm formation introduces collective agency. Individual cells attach to surfaces via flagella and pili, irreversibly adhere, produce extracellular polymeric substance matrix, mature into architecturally organized communities with water channels and nutrient gradients, and eventually disperse. The quorum-sensing mechanism — N-acyl homoserine lactone autoinducers accumulating until the LuxR threshold is crossed — is interpreted as inter-agent SelfDesc: the autoinducer encodes each cell's identity and state; detection by neighbors is the reading of that self-description. Biofilm-embedded bacteria are 100–1000 times more antibiotic-resistant than planktonic counterparts, in part because the biofilm converts individual spatial agency into collective persistence — a sector-mixing phenomenon. Horizontal gene transfer (conjugation, transformation, transduction) extends agency to the information level: genetic material displaces through the population, maintains sequence integrity, and couples to environmental selection. This informational agency produces the network phylogeny that distinguishes Bacteria from the tree-like persistence archetype of Archaea.
