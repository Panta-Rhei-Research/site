The human body replaces approximately 98% of its atoms annually. The gut epithelium turns over every 3–5 days; red blood cells in 120 days; the skeleton over roughly 10 years. At no point during this material flux does the organism cease to be itself. The Ship of Theseus paradox — does an object that has had all its parts replaced remain the same object? — is not a puzzle about ships. It is a puzzle about the nature of identity, and the SelfDesc framework resolves it without ambiguity. SelfDesc is defined over the ω-germ code, which is a profinite algebraic object carried by but not identical to the physical substrate. When DNA polymerase replicates the genome, every atom in the daughter strand is new; the code is unchanged. Identity is preserved when three conditions hold throughout any replacement: the code is invariant, the evaluator is continuously deformed but never absent, and SelfDesc is maintained at every instant during the transition. Hobbes's variant — where the discarded planks are reassembled into a second ship — is resolved immediately: the reassembled ship failed condition (iii), because the planks sat inert with no evaluator reading the code. Metamorphosis provides the strongest biological test of the code-level theory: during holometabolous pupation, 85–95% of larval tissues dissolve, yet the ω-germ code is unchanged in every surviving cell, the mushroom body neurons maintain continuity, and memory experiments confirm that learned aversive responses survive complete carrier restructuring. The Substrate Abstraction Theorem then derives the logical endpoint: the five τ-Distinction conditions and three SelfDesc conditions are jointly necessary and sufficient for life, and no condition references a material predicate.
