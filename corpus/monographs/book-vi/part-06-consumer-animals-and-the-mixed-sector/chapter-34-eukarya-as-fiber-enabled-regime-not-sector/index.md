A recurring confusion in biological frameworks is treating a carrier technology — the cell architecture — as though it were a sector identity. This chapter corrects that conflation for the eukaryotic domain. Eukarya is a fiber-enabled regime: an internal cell architecture that internalizes the metabolic fiber T² into the carrier's endomorphism algebra, making γ and η generators into internal operations rather than boundary exchanges. The four eukaryotic innovations — the nucleus (information switching), mitochondria (energy budget), the endomembrane system (internal routing), and the cytoskeleton (intracellular agency) — are jointly necessary and jointly sufficient for this regime. The regime is sector-agnostic: plants run the source sector using eukaryotic architecture; fungi run the closure sector; animals run the consumer sector; even protists populate the persistence and agency sectors. The 1st Edition of this work implicitly treated Eukarya as a mixed sector on (α, π), which the uniqueness theorem (Chapter 33) rules out. This chapter formalizes the correction: the mixed sector is a structural role on (γ, η), and the fiber-enabled regime is a carrier capacity that enables any role. The asymmetry is one-directional — the consumer sector requires the fiber-enabled regime to sustain co-dominant winding, but the regime does not determine which sector a lineage runs. The four eukaryotic innovations (nucleus, mitochondria, endomembrane system, cytoskeleton) are jointly necessary and jointly sufficient for the regime; removing any one collapses the capacity for sector switching, internal routing, or sustained energy throughput that co-dominant winding demands. The endosymbiotic origin of mitochondria (~2 Gya) and chloroplasts (~1.5 Gya) is reinterpreted in this framework as the regime transition event: the archaeal host, previously base-restricted, acquired internal access to the full metabolic fiber and became capable of running source, closure, or consumer sectors as internally driven operations.
