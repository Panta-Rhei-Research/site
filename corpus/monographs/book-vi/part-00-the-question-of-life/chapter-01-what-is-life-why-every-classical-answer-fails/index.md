Every classical attempt to define life — Aristotle's soul, Schrödinger's negentropy, NASA's working definition — captures a necessary condition but none is sufficient. The difficulty is structural rather than empirical: every classical definition either lists properties (which admit counterexamples) or appeals to intuition (which is not a definition). Fire metabolizes but has no self/non-self boundary; mules are alive but do not reproduce; crystals grow but carry no internal code; computers process information but have no internally generated boundary. The chapter examines these four canonical failures, surveys the seven empirical hallmarks of life that biology has identified (organization, metabolism, homeostasis, growth, reproduction, response, evolution), and makes explicit their two fundamental weaknesses as a definition — they are descriptive rather than explanatory, and they do not sharply delineate the living from the non-living. The chapter then previews the programme's answer: life is defined by two structural predicates, τ-Distinction and SelfDesc, and the seven hallmarks are theorems that follow from that definition, not additional requirements. Those two predicates come from the categorical framework of Books I–V; Chapter 2 explains how.
