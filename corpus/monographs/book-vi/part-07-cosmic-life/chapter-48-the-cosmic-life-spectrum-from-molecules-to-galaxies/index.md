The Distinction/SelfDesc matrix is a 2 × 2 classifier that answers the question "Is X alive?" for every physical system. Quadrant (I) contains inert matter — gas, photons, rocks — with neither predicate satisfied. Quadrant (II) is structurally impossible: SelfDesc requires an evaluator that reconstructs the distinction, so if no distinction exists the evaluator has nothing to reconstruct. Quadrant (III) contains bounded-but-not-alive systems: they draw a self/non-self boundary but lack an internal evaluator that reads a code to reconstruct it. Quadrant (IV) contains alive systems: both predicates satisfied, all seven hallmarks following by theorem. The matrix is exhaustive and binary; it replaces the classical checklist of life properties with two structural conditions from which all classical criteria either follow or can be shown inapplicable.

Stars are the chapter's decisive case study and the clearest demonstration that distinction without SelfDesc does not constitute Life. The stellar photosphere provides a transient distinction — it satisfies clopen and refinement-coherence during the main-sequence phase — but fails eventual stability (the boundary migrates through red giant, white dwarf, and other post-main-sequence stages) and law-stability (no area theorem protects it; mass-loss rates of 10⁻¹⁴–10⁻⁵ M☉/yr progressively dissolve it). More decisively, no internal evaluator exists: nuclear fusion is a law-governed exothermic process acting on initial conditions, not an evaluator reading a stored code. The star does not aim at maintaining its boundary; the photosphere is a passive consequence of opacity, not a decoded target. Stars, neutron stars, and white dwarfs all land in quadrant (III). The contrast with black holes is absolute: the event horizon is law-stable, eventually stable, and internally maintained by the Einstein field equations reading the ω-germ code. SelfDesc is the sharp dividing predicate — the decisive boundary between bounded and alive. The organism/ecosystem/basin taxonomy then organizes quadrant-(IV) systems at three canonical scales: organisms (single carriers satisfying both predicates), ecosystems (inter-carrier morphism webs that support but are not themselves alive), and basins (gravitationally delimited regions anchored by a living carrier).
