This chapter addresses the prior question: given a universe governed by the τ-axioms with a sustained energy gradient, must the persistence sector acquire occupants? The answer is yes, and the argument has been upgraded from conjectural to τ-effective in this edition through a seven-link derivation chain. The chapter inverts the classical framing in which life is a precarious island of order requiring an improbably lucky ascent against thermodynamic pressure. In the τ-framework, life is a valley in the defect landscape: the surrounding non-living configurations are at higher free energy, and matter flows downhill toward the living configuration.

The defect-thermodynamic argument runs in three steps. First, self-maintaining structures produce entropy at higher rates than unstructured matter occupying the same energy gradient — forests dissipate energy far more efficiently than bare rock. Second, Prigogine's dissipative structures occupy local minima of the defect functional Δ: they are thermodynamically preferred configurations. Third, SelfDesc closure (ch. 07) converts these entropy-production maxima into dynamically stable attractors: the carrier's internal evaluator reconstructs the distinction after perturbation, making the basin absorbing rather than merely a minimum.

The formal core introduces the Far-From-Equilibrium Regime (VI.D74), the Complexity Budget C(n) = N − |D_n| (VI.D75), and the Complexity Monotone lemma (VI.L15), which establishes that C(n) increases monotonically as defects decay geometrically per the Master Exhaustion Inequality (V.T62). The Distinction Threshold T = 8 (five conditions for τ-Distinction plus three for SelfDesc, VI.D76) is finite; the Attractor Existence Theorem (VI.T44) then guarantees that threshold crossing is forced: there exists an orbit step n₀ at which C(n₀) ≥ T. The Absorbing Basin Lemma (VI.L16) ensures the carrier cannot subsequently exit. Together these establish that abiogenesis — the first persistence-sector entry — is structurally inevitable. The timescale bound (VI.T45, VI.D77) is logarithmic: T_abio ≤ n_{1/2} · ⌈ln(N/T)⌉, which with n_{1/2} ≈ 1.66 and a characteristic step time ~10⁻¹³ s translates to a physical window of 10²–10⁸ years, consistent with the geological evidence of life appearing within ~100–300 Myr of habitable conditions (VI.R27, VI.R08).

The chapter closes by passing the classical abiogenesis scenarios — Miller–Urey prebiotic synthesis, RNA World, metabolism-first autocatalytic cycles, alkaline hydrothermal vents — through the persistence lens. Each addresses one or more of the four conditions of the First Persistence Event definition (VI.D26): boundary formation, eventual stability, proto-evaluator, and basin return. The scenarios are not competitors but co-contributors, and the τ-framework predicts that all four conditions arose in close temporal proximity because the defect landscape funnels prebiotic chemistry toward their simultaneous satisfaction.
