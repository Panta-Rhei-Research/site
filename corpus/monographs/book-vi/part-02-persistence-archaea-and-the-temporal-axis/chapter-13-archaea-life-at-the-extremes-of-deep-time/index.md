Chapter 12 defined the persistence sector through three formal conditions — eventual stability, basin return, and law-stability — and named the domain Archaea as the biological archetype. This chapter delivers the biological case: each formal condition receives a concrete archaeal realization, and the match is not approximate but precise enough to serve as independent evidence that the persistence sector picks out a genuine structural category.

The extremophile biology section establishes the empirical foundation. Carl Woese's 16S rRNA analysis in the 1970s revealed that organisms previously grouped with bacteria were in fact as distant from Bacteria as either group is from Eukarya, giving rise to the three-domain classification. The organisms reclassified as Archaea were notable for inhabiting conditions previously considered incompatible with life: Methanopyrus kandleri grows at 122°C; Picrophilus torridus at pH 0; Halobacterium salinarum at 5 M NaCl; Thermococcus gammatolerans survives 30 kGy of ionizing radiation. This catalog (VI.R07) makes the basin-return condition empirically vivid. Thermococcus gammatolerans shatters its genome into hundreds of fragments under extreme radiation and reassembles it within hours — a textbook case of the carrier returning to its distinction after perturbation.

The distinctive membrane chemistry of Archaea — ether-linked isoprenoid chains on glycerol-1-phosphate, forming either bilayers or, at temperatures above 80°C, thermally stable monolayer tetraether structures — grounds the law-stability condition. The ether linkages resist hydrolysis under acidic, alkaline, and high-temperature conditions that destroy ester bonds; the metabolic function (methanogenesis, chemolithotrophy) remains invariant across radically different environments. The G1P glycerol stereochemistry also connects to the chapter on homochirality: archaeal and bacterial membranes are mirror-image solutions to the same compartmentalization problem, a divergence fixed at or near the origin of life.

Phylogenetic position grounds the eventual-stability condition. Three independent lines of evidence — Woese's 16S rRNA trees, the Asgard superphylum placing Eukarya within the archaeal radiation, and the reconstructed LUCA genome with its methanogenesis-like physiology — converge on the same conclusion: Archaea have changed the least since divergence from LUCA approximately 3.5 billion years ago. Eukarya are a derived branch within the archaeal tree, augmented by bacterial endosymbionts; Bacteria diversified metabolically. Archaea retained the ancestral core. The chapter closes with a section on cell-size bounds that derives both a minimum cell diameter (~0.2 μm, set by genome and ribosome packing requirements) and a maximum (~120 μm, set by the diffusion time constraint on metabolite turnover), observing that archaeal DPANN episymbionts and giant marine algae mark the respective limits.
