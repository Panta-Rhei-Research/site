The hinge block — Parts IV through VII — has transformed Category τ from a countable combinatorial object into a structured mathematical universe with coordinates, dimension, polarity, pre-topological boundary data, and an earned algebraic boundary. This chapter steps back to survey the architecture as a whole, explains the local–global bridge that makes the transformation possible, contrasts it with orthodox frameworks that lack this bridge, and previews the remaining nine parts of Book I. The bridge is this: each prime p is a finite witness — its polarity pol(p) ∈ {+, −} is a decidable local datum — yet collectively the polarity sequence over all primes generates the global algebraic lemniscate ℒ through omega-germs and the split-complex discovery. No single prime knows the full structure; each contributes one irrevocable bit.

## What this chapter contributes

The chapter articulates the local–global bridge as a characteristic feature of τ that orthodox frameworks lack. In classical number theory, primes are studied for their distribution and analytic behavior, but no canonical polarity is assigned. The reason is structural: classical ℕ has exponentiation as its highest internal operation. Without tetration as an earned internal operation, there is no η-channel to create the B/C bifurcation, and the spectral algebra collapses to a unipolar single-sector structure. Tetration's role is therefore not cosmetic: it is the operation whose internal derivation from ρ creates the second channel that splits the algebra and generates the lemniscate. The chapter presents a retrospective table of the hinge block's four parts (IV: ABCD coordinate chart; V: Hyperfactorization; VI: Prime Polarity; VII: Algebraic Lemniscate), each with its key result and downstream consequence. All results in the hinge block are ZFC-provable — statements about natural numbers, prime factorizations, growth rates, and inverse limits that hold in standard mathematics. What is new is the interpretation: these results are discovered as intrinsic properties of τ rather than imported from outside. The chapter then lists the nine remaining parts (VIII–XVI) and the specific dependencies each has on the hinge block. No new formal results are introduced; no separate Lean module is needed.
