The preceding chapters established that the temporal epoch opens at ignition depth n_ign, where H_∂[ω] first supports stable spectral modes. This chapter asks: what does the universe look like at that moment? The answer is extreme: at n_ign all spectral labels Λ_{n_ign} are active (no primorial filtering has occurred), all five sectors couple at full ι_τ-determined strength, and the τ-Einstein equation G_ω(x) = κ_τ · T^mat_ω(x), with κ_τ = 1 − ι_τ ≈ 0.659, is in its strongest regime. This is what orthodox cosmology calls the hot Big Bang—but without a singularity. The generator α₁ = α is a well-defined starting point, not a curvature divergence; H_∂[ω]|₁ is trivial (zero element only), not infinite.

Entropy at the opening is high: the holomorphic path count P_holo(n_ign, r) is maximal because all modes contribute, so S(n_ign) is large. The orthodox Past Hypothesis—the assumption of a low-entropy initial state—is not needed and is not assumed; it is replaced by the structural second law proved in Book IV. The global arrow points from high spectral energy (undifferentiated modes) toward low spectral energy (highly differentiated structure). A threshold ladder maps to the orthodox sequence: Planck era, GUT decoupling, QCD confinement, electroweak breaking, BBN, and CMB recombination all correspond to refinement depths at which specific sectors reach their characteristic coupling strength. Critically, the refinement progression rate H(n) = q_{n+1} − 1)/ℓ(Δ_n) grows super-exponentially at early depths, producing the chart-level readout that orthodox cosmology calls inflation—driven by ρ alone, with no inflaton field and no slow-roll parameters. Flatness is a theorem (T² is flat by construction); the horizon problem does not arise (all spatial points at depth n are structurally simultaneous on Σ_n).
