Time and causality are in hand; what remains is space and distance. This chapter introduces the single principle that governs all macroscopic spatial observables: distance is not a primitive but a *readout* of the boundary holonomy algebra. There is no pre-existing spatial background. A photon is a *null intertwiner* — a morphism in H_∂[ω] connecting B-sector characters at different refinement depths, carrying zero spectral mass (m²(φ) = 0) and mediated through the crossing point ω. Distance is defined operationally via the radar-time protocol: emit a null intertwiner toward a target, wait for its return, and read off half the round-trip arc length on τ¹. Cosmological redshift is *refinement drift*: a photon emitted at depth n_s and received at depth n_r has 1 + z = p_{n_r}/p_{n_s} = ∏ q_k (product of primes between the two depths). "Expansion of the universe" is a readout artefact — the scale factor a(n) = p_n/2 grows because α-ticks shrink, not because space stretches. The Hubble parameter H(n) = (q_{n+1} − 1) · p_n acquires a precise depth-dependent meaning, and the "H₀ tension" appears immediately as a regime-dependence issue.
