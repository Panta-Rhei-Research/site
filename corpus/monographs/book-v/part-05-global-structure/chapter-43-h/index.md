The Hubble constant H₀ measures the current expansion rate of the universe.
For over a decade two classes of measurement have yielded discrepant values:
"early-universe" methods (CMB analysis via Planck, BAO calibration)
give H₀ ≈ 67.4 ± 0.5 km/s/Mpc,
while "late-universe" methods
(Cepheid-calibrated Type Ia supernovae via SH0ES and related programmes)
give H₀ ≈ 73.0 ± 1.0 km/s/Mpc.
The discrepancy exceeds 5σ and has resisted resolution within the ΛCDM framework
for nearly a decade.
This chapter presents the τ-resolution.
In Category τ the Hubble parameter is not a single number.
It is an **orbit-depth-dependent readout** (*V.D70*):
the progression rate on the base τ¹ varies with the depth n of the refinement tower,
and "early" and "late" measurements probe *different depths*.
The early-universe measurement reads the progression rate at large depth
(high redshift, z ~ 1100),
while the late-universe measurement reads it at shallow depth (low redshift, z ≲ 0.1).
There is no tension: both measurements are correct.
They measure different things.
The second half of the chapter re-reads ΛCDM as a whole.
The model has correct *parameters* (Ω_m, Ω_Λ, H₀, n_s, σ₈)
but wrong *ontology*
(dark matter as particle, dark energy as field, spacetime as manifold).
The parameters are readout projections of the τ-structure,
and a complete translation table from ΛCDM to τ-language is given.
The chapter closes with the final word on the dark sector
and with falsification criteria:
specific observations that would reopen the case.
