The previous ten chapters of Part V have shown — one astrophysical system at a time —
that dark matter is *not needed*.
Rotation curves (Chapter 34), cluster mass profiles, the Bullet Cluster (Chapter 40),
and the cosmic web (Chapter 40) are all accounted for
by the boundary holonomy mass of Category τ.
This chapter makes a stronger claim.
Dark matter is not merely unnecessary: it is **structurally impossible**.
The argument is purely algebraic and rests on three premises:
the coherence kernel of Category τ has exactly five generators
{α, π, γ, η, ω} (the 5-Generator Theorem, Book I);
each generator maps to exactly one sector through the Generator–Sector Correspondence
(Book IV, Chapter 6);
and the fifth generator ω is not independent — ω = γ ∩ η
(the crossing point of the lemniscate).
The result is the **Sector Exhaustion Theorem** (*V.T44*):
the boundary holonomy algebra decomposes as
H_∂[ω] = ⊕_{X ∈ {A,B,C,D}} H_X ⊕ H_ω,
and this decomposition is exhaustive.
There is no remainder term, no hidden sector, no sixth component.
This is the Hinge Theorem (Book III, III.T41) applied to astrophysics:
the E₁ template exhausts all available structure,
and the No Knobs Theorem (III.T42) ensures that no hidden parameter
could accommodate extra matter.
The corollaries are immediate:
no dark matter particle, no dark energy field, no fifth force.
The 68% "dark energy" and 27% "dark matter" of the ΛCDM budget
are readout artifacts — projections of the τ-structure
onto a framework that has too few categories to parse them correctly.
